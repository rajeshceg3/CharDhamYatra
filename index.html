Begin the Journey

The mountains do not call; they wait. And when you finally arrive, you realize they have been waiting for you since before you were born.

The Journey Within

4 Sacred Sites | 1,607 km Circuit

The Yatra is a circumambulation of the soul. Tradition dictates the path from West to East—Yamunotri to Gangotri, Kedarnath to Badrinath—following the eternal direction of the sun.

The air here is thin, tasting of cold stone and ancient ice. It is not a destination, but a beginning—where the river is still a child, boisterous and untamed.

Yamunotri

3,293 m | The Source

Yamuna, the daughter of the Sun and sister of Yama, the God of Death. It is said that those who bathe in her waters are spared the pain of death's embrace. High above, the Saptarishi Kund remains her hidden, glacial source.

There is a silence in the roar of the river. It is the sound of the earth speaking to the sky. Here, the water carries the weight of legends, yet feels as light as memory.

Gangotri

3,100 m | The Origin

Here, King Bhagirath’s penance brought the Ganges down from the heavens. Lord Shiva caught her torrential fall in his matted locks to save the earth from destruction. The river here is Bhagirathi, pure and untamed.

To walk here is to walk into the spine of the world. The peaks rise like white giants, indifferent and magnificent. In their shadow, the temple stands—a stone prayer that has weathered storms for centuries.

Kedarnath

3,583 m | Rudraprayag District

One of the twelve Jyotirlingas. The Pandavas sought Lord Shiva here for redemption. Eluding them, he took the form of a bull and dove into the earth, leaving behind this hump—a stone prayer that has weathered storms for millennia.

The valley opens like a cupped hand offering light. It is the exhale after the climb. The colors are warmer here, the wind softer, as if the mountains themselves have decided to be kind.

Badrinath

3,133 m | Chamoli District

The sanctuary of Lord Vishnu. When he meditated here in the biting cold, his consort Goddess Laxmi took the form of a Badri (berry) tree to shelter him. It is the place of balance, guarded by the Nar and Narayana mountains.

You leave, but a part of you remains. Quiet. Still.
